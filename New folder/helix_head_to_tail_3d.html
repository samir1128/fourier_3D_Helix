What "head to tail" means in 3D helix language: Each helix e^(inωt) is not a flat circle — it is a corkscrew climbing through time. When you chain two of them head to tail, it means: the base of helix 2 is not nailed to the origin. It is nailed to the tip of helix 1 at every moment in time simultaneously. So helix 2 is itself corkscrewing, but the center of that corkscrew is itself moving along the path of helix 1's tip. Think of it like a planet-moon system extruded through time: Helix 1 (n=1) is the Earth's orbit around the Sun — a slow wide corkscrew Helix 2 (n=3) is the Moon's orbit around Earth — a tighter faster corkscrew whose axis is not fixed in space but follows helix 1's tip Helix 3 (n=5) orbits the tip of helix 2, and so on

The projection collapses the picture two ways: Look along the time axis (front view button): time disappears, and you see the 2D epicycles — the classic chained circles Look along the Im axis (side view): the imaginary part disappears, and you see Re(t) — a cosine series building up the square wave

So the 3D helix picture and the 2D epicycle picture are literally the same object — one is the full 3D truth, the other is what you see when you stand at the end of the time axis and look back. The Fourier series says: any periodic signal is the shadow of a chain of nested helices, viewed along the time axis.
